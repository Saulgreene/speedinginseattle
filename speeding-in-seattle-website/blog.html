What are the limitations on using my cell phone while driving?

September 26, 2017

Cell phones today are more than just phones, they are little computers that can be used for texting, talking, blogging, taking pictures and keeping up to date with social media. What does the State of Washington have to say about it when driving? The legislature, in an effort to curtail the use of phones while driving has instituted two seperate infractions for using your phone wile driving: RCW 46.61.667 - to not talk on your cell phone by puting up to your ear and RCW 46.61.668 - to not read or send texts. Neither of these infractions appear on your record and are essentially a monetary penalty (unless you are a CDL driver), this way the legislature was able to pass the laws and get them on the books; however, they may in the future put them on your record, because they are now a law and easier to change.

The problem is that even these laws are out of date for the times as you can still check social media or emails and the like and not violate the existing laws. The cities and now the counties have instituted local codes for Inattentive Driving, which address the issues where there is no specific law and would apply to the above, or any situation where you are not giving your full attention to the roadway (eating, no hands, taking photos). Inattention to driving, just like the above RCW's does not go on your driving record; however, there is a higher maximum penalty in some jurisdictions of $550. Please see this Seattle Times article about it: http://www.seattletimes.com/seattle-news/inattentive-driving-could-soon-cost-you-124-in-king-county/

As is best practice, it just makes sense to not get the attention of police officer's but it is good to be aware of the laws so that you know your rights.

Do I have to yield to Metro buses because of the yield sign on the back of the bus?

Sep 23, 2016

A few of my clients and friends have recently asked me this question and I see these buses daily while commuting. While the general rule of thumb is the bigger the vehicle, the more you should yield to it (general rule of cause and effect), I am going to answer the question as far as traffic infractions are concerned. The signage on the buses is deceptive, because it has the big yield sign with the language for buses and on the edge the rcw that is applicable. As a regular driver though, all you see is the yield sign and presume that you must yield to the buses.

Transit vehicles:

  1. The driver of a vehicle shall yield the right-of-way to a transit vehicle traveling in the same direction that has signalled and is reentering the traffic flow.
  2. Nothing in this section shall operate to relieve the driver of a transit vehicle from the duty to drive with due regard for the safety of all persons using the roadway.

This yield sign ONLY applies when reentering traffic flow and when the bus uses its signal. Additionally, the bus must still comply with reasonable safety. Yielding the right of way is further defined as follows: "Vehicle or pedestrian right-of-way" means the right of one vehicle or pedestrian to proceed in a lawful manner in preference to another vehicle or pedestrian approaching under such circumstances of direction, speed, and proximity as to give rise to danger of collision unless one grants precedence to the other.

Now you know the rest of the story, as Paul Harvey used to say, behind the signage on transit buses.

Driverless cars to potentially hit the streets near you soon.

Sep 21, 2016

Driverless cars are making a push into normalized society through manufacturer's and tech companies that are pursuing the technology. Just recently those innovator's are trying to use a stretch of roadway in Washongton State to the border with Canada as a potential testing ground. They are hoping that lawmakers will allow them to integrate these driverless cars into the carpool lane to start and then over a period of years to be allowed in all lanes and eventually to have a lane for themselves. Currently Tesla has vehicles that have autopilot and even with the unfortunate accidents their cars fare better than the normal vehicles that have drivers with regards to accidents and fatalities. Now that driverless cars and autopilot are more mainstream, the Federal government is now potentially looking into regulating the industry as a whole, even though health safety and welfare are reserved for the States. The car industry may actually be in favor of such legislation as the States are all over the board as to what level, if at all, they will allow such cars to drive on their roads.
